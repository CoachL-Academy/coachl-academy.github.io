Week 1: Understanding Gaslighting

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define gaslighting and understand its origins
  • Recognise the different forms gaslighting takes
  • Understand how gaslighting affects your sense of reality
  • Begin to identify gaslighting in your own experience

📖 Key Concepts

1. What is Gaslighting?

Gaslighting is a form of psychological manipulation where someone causes you to question your own memory, perception, and sanity. The term comes from the 1944 film Gaslight, where a husband manipulates his wife into thinking she is going insane.

  • It is deliberate — designed to make you doubt yourself
  • It erodes your confidence in your own reality
  • It occurs in romantic relationships, families, workplaces, and friendships
  • Over time it becomes difficult to trust your own judgment

2. Common Gaslighting Phrases

  • "That never happened" — denying events you clearly remember
  • "You're too sensitive" — invalidating your emotional response
  • "You're imagining things" — questioning your perception
  • "You're crazy" — attacking your mental stability
  • "Everyone agrees with me, not you" — isolating you
  • "You always do this" — rewriting your character
  • "I was just joking" — minimising harmful behaviour

3. Types of Gaslighting

  • Trivialising: Making your feelings seem unimportant or excessive
  • Diverting: Changing the subject to avoid accountability
  • Blocking/Forgetting: Refusing to engage or pretending not to remember
  • Countering: Questioning your memory even when you are certain
  • Withholding: Pretending not to understand to avoid conversation

4. Why It Is So Damaging

Gaslighting is insidious because it attacks the very tool you use to protect yourself — your mind. When you can no longer trust your own perceptions, you become dependent on the gaslighter to define reality for you.

  • You begin apologising constantly, even when you have done nothing wrong
  • You make excuses for the gaslighter's behaviour
  • You feel confused and "foggy" much of the time
  • Your self-esteem steadily erodes

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Phrase Journal
Write down any phrases from the list above that sound familiar. Next to each one, write a memory of when you heard it and how it made you feel.

Exercise 2: Reality Check List
Write five things you know to be true about yourself that no one can take away — your values, your strengths, a memory only you hold.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • When did you first start doubting your own memory?
  • Which gaslighting phrases feel most familiar to you?
  • How has your confidence in your own perception changed over time?
  • What do you wish someone had told you earlier?

Week 2: The Impact on Your Identity

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand how prolonged gaslighting reshapes your identity
  • Recognise the signs of identity erosion
  • Learn how gaslighting creates emotional dependency
  • Begin reconnecting with who you were before the gaslighting

📖 Key Concepts

1. Identity Erosion

When someone consistently tells you that your feelings, memories, and perceptions are wrong, you begin to internalise their version of reality. Over time your sense of self is replaced by a version shaped entirely by the gaslighter.

  • You stop voicing opinions to avoid conflict
  • You adopt their preferences, interests, and values
  • You can no longer remember who you were before the relationship
  • You define yourself through their eyes rather than your own

2. The Inner Critic

One of the most lasting effects of gaslighting is the internalised critical voice. The gaslighter's words become your own self-talk:

  • "I'm too sensitive"
  • "I always make things worse"
  • "I can't trust myself"
  • "Maybe I really am the problem"

Recognising this voice as theirs — not yours — is a crucial step in healing.

3. Emotional Dependency

Gaslighting creates a cycle of dependency. Because you can no longer trust your own perception, you turn to the gaslighter to validate your reality — giving them enormous power over your emotional state.

  • You feel anxious without their approval
  • Their mood determines your mood
  • You constantly monitor their reactions for clues about how to feel
  • Moments of kindness from them feel like enormous relief

4. Who Were You Before?

Healing begins by remembering. Think back to who you were before this relationship:

  • What did you enjoy? What made you laugh?
  • What were your opinions, values, and dreams?
  • How did you carry yourself? What were you proud of?
  • Who were your people?

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Inner Critic Audit
Write down your most common self-critical thoughts. Next to each one, write whose voice that actually sounds like. Is it yours — or theirs?

Exercise 2: The Before Letter
Write a letter to your current self from the version of you that existed before. What would that person say to you right now?

📝 Reflection Questions

  • When did you start losing confidence in your own opinions?
  • Which parts of yourself did you give up to keep the peace?
  • What does your inner critic say most often? Whose voice is it?
  • What is one quality you had before that you want to reclaim?

Week 3: Rebuilding Self-Trust

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand why self-trust breaks down under gaslighting
  • Learn practical strategies to rebuild trust in your own perceptions
  • Develop tools to validate your own reality
  • Begin making decisions confidently again

📖 Key Concepts

1. Why Self-Trust Breaks Down

Self-trust is built through a cycle: you perceive something, you act on that perception, and the outcome validates your judgment. Gaslighting disrupts every stage of this cycle. When someone consistently tells you your perceptions are wrong, you stop relying on them entirely.

2. The Evidence Journal

One of the most powerful tools for rebuilding self-trust is documentation. When you write things down as they happen, you create an external record that cannot be rewritten by someone else.

  • Record events with dates, what was said, and how you felt
  • Note physical sensations — your body often knows before your mind does
  • Review your entries regularly to recognise patterns
  • Trust your documentation over their denials

3. Rebuilding Your Internal Compass

Your gut feelings did not disappear — they were suppressed. To rebuild your internal compass:

  • Start small: Make low-stakes decisions without seeking approval
  • Notice physical cues: Tightness in your chest, unease in your stomach are data
  • Celebrate being right: Every time your instinct proves accurate, acknowledge it
  • Stop over-explaining: You do not need to justify your feelings to anyone

4. The "Would I Tell a Friend?" Test

When you doubt yourself, ask: if a trusted friend told me this happened to them, would I believe them? The answer is almost always yes. Apply that same belief to yourself.

5. Seeking Outside Perspective

Trusted friends, family, or a therapist can serve as a reality check while you rebuild. Choose people who have no connection to the gaslighter and who have shown they respect your perspective.

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Evidence Journal
Start a private journal today. For one week, document your experiences exactly as they happen. Resist the urge to edit or second-guess your entries.

Exercise 2: The Small Decisions Challenge
Each day this week, make three decisions without asking for anyone's opinion — what to eat, what to wear, what to watch. Notice how it feels.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • When do you most distrust your own perception?
  • Can you remember a time your gut feeling was right that you ignored?
  • What would it feel like to fully trust yourself again?
  • Who in your life validates your reality without agenda?

Week 4: Setting Boundaries After Gaslighting

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand why boundaries are especially difficult for gaslighting survivors
  • Learn how to identify what your boundaries actually are
  • Practice communicating boundaries clearly and calmly
  • Manage the guilt and pushback that comes with boundary-setting

📖 Key Concepts

1. Why Boundaries Feel Impossible

Gaslighting conditions you to believe that your needs are unreasonable. Every time you tried to assert a boundary it was met with denial, ridicule, or punishment. Over time, you stopped trying. Now boundary-setting feels terrifying — and that makes complete sense.

2. Identifying Your Boundaries

Before you can communicate a boundary, you need to know what it is. Ask yourself:

  • What interactions leave me feeling drained, ashamed, or confused?
  • What behaviours do I tolerate that I deeply do not want to?
  • When do I feel my values are being compromised?
  • What would I never allow if I fully believed I deserved respect?

3. The Boundary Formula

Keep it simple and factual:

  • State the behaviour: "When you raise your voice at me..."
  • State your response: "...I will leave the room."
  • Follow through — every time.

You do not need to explain, justify, or apologise for your boundaries. A boundary is not a negotiation.

4. Handling the Guilt

Gaslighting survivors almost always feel guilty when they set a boundary. This guilt was installed deliberately — it kept you compliant. Recognise it for what it is:

  • Guilt about having needs is not a sign your needs are wrong
  • Their anger at your boundary is not evidence your boundary is unfair
  • You are not responsible for managing their reaction to your boundary

5. No JADE

Do not Justify, Argue, Defend, or Explain your boundaries to someone who has gaslighted you. Every explanation becomes material for further manipulation.

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Boundary Inventory
List five situations where you currently feel your boundaries are being violated. For each one, write what you would say if you fully believed you deserved respect.

Exercise 2: The Guilt Letter
Write a letter to your guilt. Acknowledge it. Then tell it that it was given to you by someone else and you are choosing to return it.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What happens in your body when you try to say no?
  • Whose reaction are you most afraid of when you set a boundary?
  • What boundary, if you held it firmly, would change your life most?
  • What do you need to believe about yourself to set that boundary?

Week 5: Processing the Grief

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the unique grief that comes with gaslighting recovery
  • Recognise the stages of grief in your own healing journey
  • Learn to grieve the relationship you thought you had
  • Develop healthy ways to move through grief without suppressing it

📖 Key Concepts

1. The Grief That Nobody Talks About

Recovering from gaslighting involves a grief that is hard to explain to others. You are not only grieving the relationship — you are grieving:

  • The person you thought they were (who never truly existed)
  • The relationship you believed you had
  • The years you invested based on a false reality
  • The version of yourself you lost during that time
  • The future you imagined together

2. The Stages of Grief in Recovery

  • Shock and Denial: "This can't have been deliberate"
  • Anger: "How could they do this to me?" — a healthy and necessary stage
  • Bargaining: "What if I had handled things differently?"
  • Depression: The full weight of the loss settles in
  • Acceptance: Understanding what happened without needing it to have been different

These stages are not linear. You may move between them many times.

3. Allowing Yourself to Grieve

Many survivors feel they have no right to grieve — after all, the relationship was not what they thought. But your grief is real. The love you felt was real even if it was not reciprocated honestly. Give yourself permission to mourn.

4. Grief vs Rumination

There is a difference between processing grief and ruminating. Healthy grief moves — it rises, peaks, and passes. Rumination loops — the same thoughts cycling without resolution.

  • If you are crying and feeling — you are grieving. Let it happen.
  • If you are replaying conversations trying to find different outcomes — that is rumination. Gently redirect yourself.

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Grief List
Write down everything you are grieving — the relationship, the person, the time, the self you lost. Be specific. Naming your losses is the first step to releasing them.

Exercise 2: The Anger Letter
Write an uncensored letter to the person who gaslighted you. Say everything you never could. You do not send this letter — it is for you alone.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What are you finding hardest to grieve?
  • Which stage of grief feels most present for you right now?
  • Do you give yourself permission to be angry? Why or why not?
  • What would it mean to reach acceptance?

Week 6: Reclaiming Your Reality & Moving Forward

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what full recovery from gaslighting looks like
  • Build a personalised healing plan
  • Learn to recognise gaslighting in future relationships
  • Step into your reclaimed identity with confidence

📖 Key Concepts

1. What Recovery Actually Looks Like

Recovery is not the absence of pain — it is the return of your own voice. You will know you are healing when:

  • You trust your own perception more often than you doubt it
  • You feel your feelings without immediately questioning if they are valid
  • You can say no without the world collapsing
  • You recognise manipulation earlier and respond instead of absorbing it
  • You feel like yourself again — curious, opinionated, alive

2. Protecting Yourself Going Forward

Red flags to watch for in new relationships:

  • Consistent denial of things you clearly experienced
  • Minimising your emotions: "You're overreacting"
  • Blame-shifting: Every conflict becomes your fault
  • Isolation tactics: Creating distance between you and your support system
  • Moving too fast: Intensity early on to create dependency

3. Green Flags in Healthy Relationships

  • They say "I hear you" and mean it
  • They take responsibility without being prompted
  • Disagreements are resolved — not weaponised
  • You feel more like yourself around them, not less
  • Their words and actions are consistent over time

4. Your Reclaimed Identity

You are not who the gaslighting made you believe you were. You are the person who survived it, who sought understanding, who did this work. That person is remarkable.

  • Reconnect with the things you love that were mocked or discouraged
  • Voice your opinions — even the unpopular ones
  • Trust your instincts as data worth listening to
  • Celebrate every moment of self-trust, no matter how small

5. Ongoing Self-Care

  • Consider a trauma-informed therapist if you have not already
  • Maintain your evidence journal as a self-trust practice
  • Build a support network of people who validate your reality
  • Be patient — healing from gaslighting takes time and it is non-linear

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Reclaimed Self Portrait
Write a description of who you are now that you are healing — your values, your strengths, what you will no longer accept, what you are building toward.

Exercise 2: Letter to Your Future Self
Write a letter to yourself one year from now. What do you hope they have — in their life, in their relationships, in their relationship with themselves?

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What does your reclaimed identity look like?
  • Which red flags will you never dismiss again?
  • What is the most important thing you have learned through this course?
  • What do you want to tell someone who is where you were at Week 1?

You have completed Healing from Gaslighting. You came here questioning your own reality — you leave with the tools to reclaim it. Your perception is valid. Your feelings are real. Your story is true. Keep going.
