📹 Week 1: Introduction to Narcissistic Abuse

Understanding Narcissism Basics
Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Week 1: Introduction to Narcissistic Abuse

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what narcissistic personality disorder is
  • Differentiate between healthy narcissism and pathological narcissism
  • Recognize the spectrum of narcissistic behavior
  • Learn about different types of narcissists

📖 Key Concepts

1. What is Narcissism?

Narcissism exists on a spectrum from healthy self-esteem to pathological narcissistic personality disorder (NPD). Understanding this spectrum helps you identify when behavior crosses the line from confidence into manipulation and abuse.

  • Healthy Narcissism: Appropriate self-esteem, ability to accept criticism, empathy for others
  • Narcissistic Traits: Self-centered behavior without meeting full NPD criteria
  • Narcissistic Personality Disorder: Pervasive pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy

2. Types of Narcissists

  • Overt/Grandiose Narcissist: Loud, boastful, demands attention and admiration openly
  • Covert/Vulnerable Narcissist: Appears shy or modest but harbors same sense of superiority
  • Malignant Narcissist: Most dangerous type; combines narcissism with antisocial traits
  • Communal Narcissist: Gains admiration through appearing selfless and charitable

3. Core Characteristics of NPD

  • Grandiose sense of self-importance
  • Preoccupation with fantasies of success, power, or beauty
  • Belief they are "special" and unique
  • Need for excessive admiration
  • Sense of entitlement
  • Interpersonally exploitative
  • Lack of empathy
  • Envious of others or believes others are envious of them
  • Arrogant behaviors or attitudes

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Self-Assessment
Reflect on your relationship. Write down behaviors that concerned you initially but you dismissed or rationalized away.

Exercise 2: Pattern Recognition
Create a timeline of your relationship. Note when behaviors changed and what triggered those changes.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What first attracted you to this person?
  • When did you first notice something was "off"?
  • Have you seen patterns of behavior repeat?
  • How has your self-esteem changed since the relationship began?

📹 Week 2: Identifying Narcissistic Patterns

Understanding Narcissistic Patterns
Gaslighting Explained

Week 2: Identifying Narcissistic Patterns

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Recognize common manipulation tactics used by narcissists
  • Understand the cycle of narcissistic abuse
  • Identify gaslighting techniques and their effects
  • Learn to document patterns of behavior

📖 Key Concepts

1. Common Manipulation Tactics

Narcissists employ various manipulation techniques to maintain control:

  • Love Bombing: Excessive attention and affection in the beginning to create dependency
  • Triangulation: Bringing a third person into the relationship dynamic to create jealousy
  • Silent Treatment: Withholding communication as punishment
  • Projection: Accusing you of behaviors they themselves exhibit
  • Word Salad: Confusing circular conversations that leave you disoriented
  • Future Faking: Making promises about the future they never intend to keep
  • Hoovering: Attempts to "suck you back in" after a discard

2. The Narcissistic Abuse Cycle

Understanding the cycle helps you recognize patterns:

  • Idealization Phase: You're put on a pedestal; everything feels perfect. They mirror your values and interests.
  • Devaluation Phase: Criticism begins; you can't do anything right. They systematically tear down your self-esteem.
  • Discard Phase: You're rejected or abandoned, often suddenly and cruelly.
  • Hoover Phase: They attempt to pull you back in with promises of change, love bombing, or inducing guilt.

3. Gaslighting Recognition

Gaslighting makes you question your reality. Common phrases include:

  • "That never happened" (denying events you clearly remember)
  • "You're too sensitive" (invalidating your feelings)
  • "You're crazy/imagining things" (questioning your sanity)
  • "Everyone thinks you're the problem" (isolating you)
  • "You're remembering it wrong" (rewriting history)
  • "I never said that" (denying their own words)
  • "You're overreacting" (minimizing abuse)

4. Effects of Gaslighting

  • Constant self-doubt
  • Second-guessing your memories
  • Difficulty making decisions
  • Feeling like you're "going crazy"
  • Apologizing constantly
  • Making excuses for their behavior
  • Loss of identity and confidence

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Pattern Documentation
Keep a daily journal noting interactions that made you feel confused, upset, or manipulated. Look for recurring patterns. Include dates, what was said, and how you felt.

Exercise 2: Reality Check
When you doubt yourself, write down the facts of what happened. Compare your account with any evidence (texts, emails). Trust your documentation over their denials.

Exercise 3: Manipulation Identification
Review the manipulation tactics list. Circle the ones you've experienced. This helps validate that what you're experiencing has a name and pattern.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • Which manipulation tactics have you experienced most frequently?
  • Where are you in the abuse cycle currently?
  • What gaslighting phrases sound most familiar to you?
  • How has this affected your sense of reality?
  • When did you first notice these patterns?

📹 Week 3: Understanding Trauma Bonds

Breaking Trauma Bonds
Why You Can't Just Leave

Week 3: Understanding Trauma Bonds

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what trauma bonding is and how it forms
  • Recognize the signs of trauma bonding in your relationship
  • Learn the psychology behind why leaving is so difficult
  • Identify cognitive dissonance and how it keeps you stuck

📖 Key Concepts

1. What is Trauma Bonding?

Trauma bonding is a powerful emotional attachment that forms between an abuser and victim through cycles of abuse, devaluation, and positive reinforcement. It's not love—it's a survival mechanism.

  • Creates intense emotional dependency
  • Formed through intermittent reinforcement (unpredictable rewards and punishments)
  • Similar to addiction—literal chemical changes in the brain
  • Makes leaving feel impossible despite knowing you should

2. How Trauma Bonds Form

  • Initial Idealization: Creates the "high" you keep chasing
  • Intermittent Reinforcement: Occasional kindness after cruelty keeps you hoping
  • Isolation: Cuts you off from other support systems
  • Degradation: Lowers your self-esteem so you feel lucky they stay
  • Gaslighting: Makes you doubt your perception and depend on theirs
  • Small Kindnesses: Feel like massive gestures after abuse

3. Signs You're Trauma Bonded

  • You keep returning despite knowing you shouldn't
  • You make excuses for their behavior to others and yourself
  • You feel you can't live without them
  • You obsess about them constantly when apart
  • You feel euphoric during the brief good times
  • You defend them when others point out red flags
  • You believe you're the only one who truly understands them
  • You feel protective of them despite the harm they cause
  • You crave their validation and approval desperately

4. Cognitive Dissonance

Cognitive dissonance is holding two conflicting beliefs simultaneously:

  • "They love me" vs. "They hurt me repeatedly"
  • "I know I should leave" vs. "But what if they change?"
  • "They're terrible to me" vs. "But they can be so wonderful"

Your brain tries to resolve this conflict by minimizing the abuse, making excuses, or blaming yourself.

5. The Biochemistry of Trauma Bonding

  • Dopamine: Released during love bombing and intermittent rewards—highly addictive
  • Oxytocin: Bonding hormone released during any intimacy, even after abuse
  • Cortisol: Stress hormone kept chronically elevated, affecting judgment
  • Adrenaline: The "high" of the drama becomes addictive

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: The Good/Bad List
Create two columns. List all the good times and all the bad times. Be honest. Look at the ratio. Notice which list is longer.

Exercise 2: If a Friend...
Write down what you would tell a friend experiencing your exact situation. Would you tell them to stay? Read this advice to yourself daily.

Exercise 3: No Contact Preparation
Begin planning what no contact would look like. Who would support you? What would you need? Where would you live?

📝 Reflection Questions

  • Do you feel addicted to this person?
  • What excuses do you make for staying?
  • When was the last time you felt genuinely happy?
  • What are you afraid would happen if you left?
  • Which version of them do you keep hoping will return?

📹 Week 4: Setting Boundaries & Self-Protection

Establishing Firm Boundaries
Protecting Your Energy

Week 4: Setting Boundaries & Self-Protection

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what healthy boundaries are
  • Learn how to identify your boundaries
  • Practice communicating boundaries effectively
  • Develop strategies to protect yourself emotionally and physically

📖 Key Concepts

1. What Are Healthy Boundaries?

Boundaries are limits that protect your physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual well-being. They define where you end and others begin.

  • Physical Boundaries: Your body, personal space, privacy
  • Emotional Boundaries: Your feelings, ability to say no
  • Mental Boundaries: Your thoughts, values, opinions
  • Material Boundaries: Your money, possessions, resources
  • Time Boundaries: How you spend your time and energy

2. Why Narcissists Violate Boundaries

  • They don't see you as separate from them
  • Your boundaries threaten their control
  • They feel entitled to access you however they want
  • They view boundary-setting as a personal attack
  • They need to prove they're above rules that apply to others

3. How to Identify Your Boundaries

Ask yourself these questions:

  • What makes me feel uncomfortable, resentful, or drained?
  • What behaviors do I tolerate that I shouldn't?
  • When do I feel my values are being compromised?
  • What am I afraid to say no to?
  • Where do I feel taken advantage of?

4. Communicating Boundaries

The Boundary Formula:

  • Clear Statement: "I'm not comfortable with..."
  • Consequence: "If you continue to..., I will..."
  • Follow Through: Actually enforce the consequence

Examples:

  • "I'm not comfortable being yelled at. If you continue to raise your voice, I will leave the room."
  • "I won't discuss this topic anymore. If you bring it up again, I'll end the conversation."
  • "I need privacy when I'm on the phone. If you continue to interrupt, I'll take my calls elsewhere."

5. What to Expect When You Set Boundaries

  • Pushback: They'll test your boundaries repeatedly
  • Guilt: "You're being unreasonable/selfish/cruel"
  • Rage: Anger at your "defiance"
  • Love Bombing: Sudden niceness to make you drop boundaries
  • Gaslighting: "I never did that" or "You're overreacting"
  • Silent Treatment: Punishment for standing up for yourself

6. Protecting Yourself

  • Grey Rock Method: Be boring and unresponsive (if you must interact)
  • No JADE: Don't Justify, Argue, Defend, or Explain
  • Document Everything: Keep records of violations
  • Build Support System: People who validate your reality
  • Safety Planning: Know your exit strategy
  • Self-Care: Prioritize your physical and mental health

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Boundary Inventory
List 10 boundaries you need to set or reinforce. Start with the easiest and work toward harder ones.

Exercise 2: Practice Scripts
Write out and rehearse boundary statements. Practice saying them out loud until they feel natural.

Exercise 3: Consequence Planning
For each boundary, decide what consequence you'll enforce if it's violated. Make sure you're willing to follow through.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What boundaries have been violated most in your relationship?
  • Why do you find it hard to set boundaries?
  • What are you afraid will happen if you enforce boundaries?
  • Which boundaries feel most urgent to establish?
  • Who in your life respects your boundaries?

📹 Week 5: Planning Your Exit & Going No Contact

Creating a Safe Exit Plan
No Contact Explained

Week 5: Planning Your Exit & Going No Contact

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand why no contact is crucial for healing
  • Learn how to safely plan and execute an exit strategy
  • Prepare for common manipulation tactics during separation
  • Develop coping strategies for the challenges of no contact

📖 Key Concepts

1. Why No Contact is Essential

No contact (NC) means completely cutting off all communication with the narcissist. This includes:

  • No phone calls, texts, or emails
  • Blocking on all social media
  • No checking their social media
  • No contact through mutual friends
  • No "just one more conversation"

Why it's necessary:

  • Breaks the trauma bond
  • Allows your brain to detox from the addiction
  • Prevents further manipulation
  • Gives you space to heal and think clearly
  • Protects you from hoovering attempts

2. Safety Planning

Before You Leave:

  • Gather important documents (birth certificates, passports, financial records)
  • Secure your own money if finances are shared
  • Document evidence of abuse (texts, emails, photos)
  • Pack an emergency bag and store it somewhere safe
  • Identify safe places you can go
  • Tell trusted friends/family of your plan
  • Change passwords on accounts they might access
  • Consider legal protection (restraining order if needed)

3. The Exit Strategy

Choose Your Method:

  • Direct Confrontation: Only if safe. Keep it brief. Don't JADE.
  • Letter/Message: State your decision clearly, then block immediately
  • Disappear: If safety is a concern, leave without warning

Key Principles:

  • Don't seek closure from them—closure comes from within
  • Don't try to make them understand—they won't/can't
  • Keep it simple: "This relationship isn't working for me. I'm done."
  • Don't list reasons—they'll just argue or use it to manipulate
  • Have support people present if you must have final interaction

4. What to Expect After

They May Try:

  • Hoovering: Attempts to suck you back in (love bombing, apologies, promises)
  • Smear Campaign: Telling others lies about you
  • Flying Monkeys: Getting others to contact you on their behalf
  • Rage: Angry outbursts, threats, intimidation
  • Self-Pity: "I'll change," "I need you," suicide threats
  • New Supply: Flaunting a new relationship to make you jealous

5. Maintaining No Contact

Strategies:

  • Block on everything immediately
  • Change your number if needed
  • Vary your routine so you don't "run into" them
  • Have accountability partner who supports no contact
  • Write down all the bad times to read when you're tempted
  • Remind yourself: Every day of NC is a day of healing
  • Use urge surfing techniques when tempted to reach out

6. Modified Contact (If Necessary)

If you share children or cannot go full no contact:

  • Use communication apps designed for co-parents (like OurFamilyWizard)
  • Keep all communication in writing
  • Use Grey Rock method exclusively
  • Only discuss children or necessary logistics
  • Don't respond to baiting or personal attacks
  • Have a third party handle exchanges if possible

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Safety Checklist
Complete the safety planning checklist. Check off items as you accomplish them. Don't rush—prioritize your safety.

Exercise 2: Support Team
List 5 people who will support you through no contact. Contact them now and explain you'll need their help.

Exercise 3: Temptation List
Write down all the reasons you need to stay no contact. Keep this list accessible for weak moments.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What makes you most afraid to leave?
  • What resources do you need to leave safely?
  • Who will support you?
  • What might tempt you to break no contact?
  • What do you hope your life will look like in 6 months?

📹 Week 6: Healing & Moving Forward

Rebuilding Your Life
Self-Care & Recovery

Week 6: Healing & Moving Forward

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the stages of healing from narcissistic abuse
  • Learn strategies for rebuilding self-esteem and identity
  • Develop a comprehensive self-care plan
  • Recognize and prevent future narcissistic relationships

📖 Key Concepts

1. Stages of Healing

Healing isn't linear—you may move back and forth between stages:

  • Denial/Shock: "Did this really happen?" Initial disbelief
  • Grief: Mourning the person you thought they were and the relationship you hoped for
  • Anger: Rage at what was done to you (this is healthy!)
  • Bargaining: "What if I had done things differently?"
  • Depression: The weight of what happened settles in
  • Acceptance: Understanding what happened wasn't your fault
  • Integration: Incorporating lessons learned into your life
  • Transformation: Using your experience to grow stronger

2. Rebuilding Your Identity

Narcissistic abuse often leaves you not knowing who you are anymore:

  • Reconnect with yourself: What did you enjoy before? Who were you?
  • Explore new interests: Try things they discouraged or mocked
  • Journal: "Who am I?" "What do I value?" "What do I want?"
  • Set personal goals: Things for YOU, not to impress anyone
  • Practice decision-making: Start small—choose your own meal, movie, activity
  • Voice your opinions: Practice saying what you think and feel

3. Rebuilding Self-Esteem

  • Challenge negative self-talk: Would you say this to a friend?
  • List your strengths: Write 20 positive things about yourself
  • Celebrate small wins: Made it through a day? That's huge!
  • Positive affirmations: "I am worthy. I am enough. I deserve respect."
  • Avoid comparing: Your journey is your own
  • Surround yourself with positivity: People who build you up, not tear you down

4. Self-Care is Not Selfish

Physical Self-Care:

  • Prioritize sleep—your body needs to heal
  • Eat nourishing foods
  • Move your body (yoga, walks, dancing)
  • Get outside in nature
  • Limit alcohol and other substances

Emotional Self-Care:

  • Allow yourself to feel without judgment
  • Cry when you need to
  • Express anger in healthy ways (journaling, exercise, therapy)
  • Set boundaries with people who don't support your healing
  • Join support groups—you're not alone

Mental Self-Care:

  • Limit news and social media if overwhelming
  • Read books about recovery
  • Practice mindfulness and meditation
  • Challenge cognitive distortions
  • Consider therapy (look for trauma-informed or abuse specialist)

5. Red Flags to Watch For

Protect yourself from future narcissistic relationships:

  • Love bombing: Too much too soon
  • Moving too fast: "Soulmate" within weeks
  • Mirroring: They seem "perfect" for you
  • Disrespecting boundaries: "But I love you so much I can't help it"
  • Lack of empathy: Dismissive of your feelings
  • Arrogance/entitlement: Rules don't apply to them
  • Bad-mouthing exes: Everyone before you was "crazy"
  • Isolation attempts: Don't want you seeing friends/family
  • Inconsistency: Words don't match actions

6. Building Healthy Relationships

Green flags to look for:

  • Respects your boundaries
  • Takes responsibility for mistakes
  • Shows consistent behavior over time
  • Encourages your growth and independence
  • Communicates openly and honestly
  • Shows genuine empathy
  • Wants you to maintain other relationships
  • Actions match their words
  • Respects your "no"

7. Your New Life

You are not broken. You are healing. You survived. Now it's time to thrive.

  • Be patient with yourself: Healing takes time
  • Celebrate progress: Even tiny steps forward matter
  • Practice self-compassion: Treat yourself with kindness
  • Keep learning: Knowledge is power
  • Help others when ready: Your story can help someone else
  • Trust the process: You will feel okay again

💡 Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Future Self Letter
Write a letter to yourself one year from now. What do you hope your life looks like? What will you be proud of accomplishing?

Exercise 2: Rediscovery Journal
Each day this week, try one thing you used to enjoy or one new thing. Write about the experience.

Exercise 3: Self-Care Plan
Create a detailed self-care plan covering physical, emotional, and mental health. Schedule specific self-care activities weekly.

📝 Reflection Questions

  • What stage of healing do you think you're in?
  • What parts of yourself do you want to rediscover?
  • What does self-care mean to you?
  • What red flags will you never ignore again?
  • What are you most looking forward to in your new life?

🎉 Congratulations!

You've completed the Understanding Narcissistic Abuse course. You now have the knowledge and tools to protect yourself, heal, and build a beautiful life. Remember: You deserve love, respect, and happiness. Keep going—you've got this!
